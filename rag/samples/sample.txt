Cognitive Bias in Source Verification: A Randomised Controlled Trial

Abstract — This paper reports a randomised controlled trial with 500 participants over 6 months investigating how independent source verification affects the rate of false-positive claims in self-reported research summaries. Across three intervention arms we observe a 40% reduction in false positives when reviewers cross-check claims against the original source material, and a 28% accuracy gain when cross-referencing is applied across multiple sources.

1. Introduction
Source verification — the practice of confirming a claim against the original passage that supports it — has long been treated as a baseline expectation in academic publishing. Yet in the day-to-day workflow of researchers using AI assistants, claims are routinely surfaced without provenance, and downstream readers are left to estimate confidence on their own. This study quantifies the effect of mandatory source verification on the reliability of summarised research.

2. Methodology
Participants (n = 500) were recruited from three universities and assigned to one of three intervention arms. Arm A received no verification tooling; Arm B received tooling that linked each AI-summarised claim back to a single retrieved passage; Arm C received tooling that additionally cross-referenced the claim against two independent sources. Daily self-reporting metrics were collected and verified against clinical outcomes recorded by independent reviewers.

3. Results
Across all three arms, the introduction of source verification by independent reviewers reduced false-positive claims by 40% relative to baseline self-report. The Arm C condition additionally yielded a 28% improvement in claim accuracy compared to Arm B, demonstrating that cross-referencing across multiple sources is more effective than single-source verification alone. Sensitivity analysis confirmed robustness against the choice of prior; the effect held across all participant subgroups.

4. Limitations
Two limitations apply. First, the participant pool was geographically concentrated (three universities, all in similar climates), so generalisation to broader populations is unverified. Second, the demographic profile was homogeneous on age and academic stage. Future replication across more diverse cohorts is recommended.

5. Conclusion
Mandatory source verification — particularly multi-source cross-referencing — measurably improves the reliability of AI-summarised research. The 40% false-positive reduction and 28% accuracy gain suggest that verification UI should be a default feature of research assistants rather than an optional add-on.
